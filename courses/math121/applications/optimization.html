Optimization

Often one wants to solve a problem involving "optimizing" a particular quantity: finding under what circumstances it is largest or smallest. We see that calculus can help us in this endeavour, as the first derivative (in particular the Extreme Value Theorem) can be used to locate absolute maxima/minima.

Word problems present the additional challenge that we must first extract the "mathematics" (variables, functions, etc.) from a paragraph. A good general set of steps to follow is:

  • Understand the problem. Read the problem. Then read it again. Then again. You may have to read the problem through in its entirety 4 or 5 times before you can confidently say "I understand what I'm trying to do." I can't stress enough the importance of taking it slow at this stage. Often people are inclined to skim the paragraph quickly and underline all the numbers, then stare at it thinking "What am I supposed to do?" This approach tends to lead one down an incorrect path and wastes significant time.
  • Draw a picture. Often, understanding the problem would be easier if you drew a picture: especially if there's some geometry involved. This step goes hand-in-hand with the previous one. Leave room to label edges in your diagram for when you later use "let" statements to assign names to the variables.
  • Introduce notation. Assign a name to the quantity we seek to mazimize or minimize (say, \(Q\)). Also use "let" statements to assign names to other quantities in the problem. Try to use intuitive variable names, like \(h\) for height and \(A\) for area, to make things easier to understand.
  • Express \(Q\) in terms of the other variables.
  • If \(Q\) has been expressed in terms of more than 1 variable, then use the relationships between the variables to express \(Q\) in terms of only a single variable. That is, \(Q\) should now be a function of one variable (say \(x\)), so \(f(x) = Q\).
  • Find the absolute maximum or minimum value for \(Q\) (use the Closed Interval Method/Extreme Value Theorem where the domain of \(f\) is a closed interval).

Now you try!
