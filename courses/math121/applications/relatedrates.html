Related Rates

Many problems involve multiple quantities which are changing over time. That is, for a quantity \(Q\), we can talk about its rate of change with respect to time, \(\frac{dQ}{dt}\). Note that since multiple rates of change (and hence derivatives) can be involved in a problem, and they can change with respect to other variables (not just time), Leibniz notation is the appropriate way of expressing things.

Word problems present the additional challenge that we must first extract the "mathematics" (variables, functions, etc.) from a paragraph. A good general set of steps to follow is:

  • Understand the problem. Read the problem. Then read it again. Then again. You may have to read the problem through in its entirety 4 or 5 times before you can confidently say "I understand what I'm trying to do." I can't stress enough the importance of taking it slow at this stage. Often people are inclined to skim the paragraph quickly and underline all the numbers, then stare at it thinking "What am I supposed to do?" This approach tends to lead one down an incorrect path and wastes significant time.
  • Draw a picture. Often, understanding the problem would be easier if you drew a picture: especially if there's some geometry involved. This step goes hand-in-hand with the previous one. Leave room to label edges in your diagram for when you later use "let" statements to assign names to the variables.
  • Introduce notation. Use "let" statements to assign names to quantities in the problem. Try to use intuitive variable names, like \(h\) for height and \(A\) for area, to make things easier to understand.
  • Lay out "what we know"/"what we want to know" in terms of derivatives (there may be some given rates of change, and some unknown rate of change).
  • Write an equation which relates the variables in the problem. Possibly, use other relationships between the variables to eliminate one or more of them via substitution.
  • Differentiate implicitly with respect to time \(t\).
  • Substitute known quantities (including rates of change) to solve for the desired unknown.
