Implicit Differentiation

Sometimes, an equation can't be solved explicitly for a variable (and result in a function), and yet we want its rate of change with respect to another variable. For example, try rearranging \[xy^2 - 3x^2 = 2 - 2y\] to solve for \(y\): you will see that what you end with isn't a function.

But all is not lost: we can still find the derivative of \(y\) with respect to \(x\), via implicit differentiation (an application of the Chain Rule). In the preceding equation, since there are only two variables, it may be clear from context that we are taking the derivative with respect to \(x\), and so can denote the derivative of \(y\) by \(y'\) unambiguously. Later, when we have more variables, we will have to specify: we are talking about the rate of change of \(y\) as what changes? Leibniz notation takes care of this: the derivative of \(y\) with respect to \(x\) is denoted by \(\frac{dy}{dx}\).

Solving the preceding problem, we differentiate both sides of the original equation with respect to \(x\): \[\implies \frac{d}{dx}(xy^2) - \frac{d}{dx}(3x^2) = \frac{d}{dx}(2) - \frac{d}{dx}(2y)\] We work our way from left-to-right. We apply the product rule to the term \(xy^2\): \[\implies \frac{d}{dx}(x)y^2 + x\frac{d}{dx}(y^2) - \frac{d}{dx}(3x^2) = \frac{d}{dx}(2) - \frac{d}{dx}(2y)\] Then differentiating \(y^2\), we will need to apply the Chain Rule, since \(y\) is a function of \(x\). You can think of \(y\) as being the inner function, and the squaring function as being the outer function. So we take the derivative of the outer function, leave \(y\) inside it, but then multiply by the derivative of the inner function: \[\implies y^2 + x(2y)(y') - \frac{d}{dx}(3x^2) = \frac{d}{dx}(2) - \frac{d}{dx}(2y)\] The next two terms are easy, since they don't contain the variable \(y\): we differentiate them as usual (the constant vanishes): \[\implies y^2 + x(2y)(y') - 6x = -\frac{d}{dx}(2y)\] Then the derivative of \(y\) is \(y'\), so: \[\implies y^2 + x(2y)(y') - 6x = -2y'\] Finally, our goal is to isolate the derivative \(y'\). We rearrange the equation so any terms containing \(y'\) are on the left-hand side: \[\implies 2xyy' + 2y' = 6x - y^2\] And then we can factor it out of the left-hand side: \[\implies (2xy+2)y' = 6x - y^2\] and finally solve for \(y'\): \[\implies y' = \frac{6x - y^2}{2xy+2}\] Now, if we want to find the equation of a tangent line, we plug the coordinates of a point in to find the slope of the tangent there. For instance, the point \((0,1)\) is on the curve (you can verify this by plugging the point into the original equation and seeing that the left-hand-side equals the right-hand-side), so we plug into the derivative to get the slope of the tangent: \[y' = \frac{6(0) - 1^2}{2(0)(1)+2} = \frac{-1}{2}\] and since we have a point on the tangent, namely \((0,1)\), we use the point-slope form of a line to get the equation of the tangent line: \[y - 1 = -\frac{1}{2}(x - 0) \implies y = -\frac{1}{2}x + 1\]
