Fractions Activity

We begin with a quick review on working with fractions. Multiplication and division are the easiest: to multiply two fractions, you multiply their numerators together, and their denominators together: \[\frac{a}{b} \cdot \frac{c}{d} = \frac{ac}{bd}\] And to divide by a fraction, you invert and then multiply: \[\frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b} \cdot \frac{d}{c} = \frac{ad}{bc}\] When dividing a non-fraction by a fraction, note that you can simply place the dividend over 1: \[a \div \frac{b}{c} = \frac{a}{1} \div \frac{b}{c} = \frac{a}{1} \cdot \frac{c}{b} = \frac{ac}{b}\] The most common mistake I see made in multiplying fractions is attempting to "distribute" over a product, leading to duplication which shouldn't be happening: \[\frac{6x^2 \cdot y}{y} \cdot \frac{y^2}{x} \neq \frac{6x^2y^2 \cdot y^3}{yx}\] To review the distributive property of multiplication over addition, see here: Multiplication Activity. For example, we do distribute the second numerator over the sum (or difference) in the first numerator: \[\frac{x^2 + 3 - y}{z} \cdot \frac{3x}{y} = \frac{3x^3 + 9x + 3xy}{zy}\] Addition and subtraction of fractions is somewhat more difficult, as we need to give them a common denominator first, and then add/subtract the numerators (the denominator stays the same): \[\frac{a}{b} + \frac{c}{b} = \frac{a+c}{b}\] We multiply the top and bottom of each fraction by the other fraction's denominator to get them to common denominators: \[\frac{a}{b} - \frac{c}{d} = \frac{ad}{bd} - \frac{cb}{db} = \frac{ad - cb}{bd}\] Finally, let us consider reducing fractions to lowest form: that is, where the greatest common divisor of the numerator and denominator is 1. When it comes to fractions not involving variables, this is relatively simple: \[\frac{24}{36} = \frac{12}{18} = \frac{6}{9} = \frac{2}{3}\] This is because we can divide the numerator and denominator by any nonzero number and obtain an equivalent fraction. That nonzero requirement has to be given some more careful thought when we are cancelling factors containing variables. For instance, when cancelling the factor \(x-2\) from the following, we require the assumption that \(x \neq 2\): \[\frac{x^2-5x+6}{x-2} = \frac{(x-2)(x-3)}{x-2} = x-3, \text{ for } x \neq 2\] In the following activity, simplify the expression to a single fraction in lowest form (except in the multiplication/division problems, which are too randomized to implement the reducing to lowest form part of the problem), including any restrictions on the variable resulting from cancelling. This activity incorporates expanding, factoring, and exponent laws: see the relevant precalculus activities (linked from the course homepage: MATH 121) to review if necessary.
