Inequalities Activity

Inequalities are challenging to work with, as some of their properties are counterintuitive. Like with equalities, we can add/subtract anything from both sides while maintaining the truth of the inequality: \[x > y \implies x + a = y + a\] However, if we multiply or divide by a negative number, or take the reciprocal, the inequality's direction is reversed: \[x > y \implies -x < -y \hspace{1cm} \text{ and } \hspace{1cm} x > y > 0 \implies \frac{1}{x} < \frac{1}{y}\] Dealing with squares is also more complicated. It is helpful to think of these problems in terms of distance: \[x^2 = 4 \implies x = -2 \text{ or } x = 2 \implies \text{dist}(x,0) = 2\] \[x^2 > 4 \implies x > 2 \text{ or } x < -2 \implies \text{dist}(x,0) > 2\] \[x^2 < 4 \implies x < 2 \text{ and } x > -2 \implies \text{dist}(x,0) < 2\] The example involving \(x^2\) closely resembles dealing with (in)equalities involving absolute value: \[|x-n| = m \implies x-n = m \text{ or } x-n = -m \implies \text{dist}(x,n) = m\] \[|x-n| > m \implies x-n > m \text{ or } x-n < -m \implies \text{dist}(x,n) > m\] \[|x-n| < m \implies x-n < m \text{ and } x-n > -m \implies \text{dist}(x,n) < m\] Finally, inequalities involving a product of factors on one side and zero on the other occur frequently in the study of calculus, and so we pay them particular attention. We need to consider the sign of the factors for various values of \(x\) to determine the sign of their product. A sign chart is helpful in this regard.

Suppose we want to solve the inequality \[3x^4+6x^3-45x^2 > 0\] We factor the left-hand side as much as possible (see Factoring Activity for a review of factoring): \[\implies 3x^2(x^2+2x-15) < 0 \implies 3x^2(x+5)(x-3) > 0\] The factor \(3x^2 = 0\) when \(x = 0\), and is positive on either side of it, since anything squared is positive. Similarly, the factor \(x+5 = 0\) when \(x = -5\), but this time changes sign on either side of it, and the factor \(x-3 = 0\) when \(x = 3\), and changes sign on either side of it. So, we can think of \(-5\) and \(3\) as the "points of interest", or as the locations at which we "chop up" the real line. So we want to consider the sign of each factor in the intervals \((-\infty,-5)\), \((-5,3)\), and \((3, \infty)\). We use a sign chart to record the sign of each factor in each of these intervals: \[ \begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|} \hline & x-3 & x+5 & 3x^2(x+5)(x-3) \\ \hline (-\infty,-5) & - & - & + \\ \hline (-5,3) & - & + & - \\ \hline (3, \infty) & + & + & + \\ \hline \end{array} \] So, \(3x^4+6x^3-45x^2 > 0\) when \(x \in (-\infty,-5) \cup (3, \infty)\).

A side note on filling in the above sign chart: we pick "test values" in each interval to determine the sign of each factor. For instance, for the row corresponding to the interval \((-5,3)\), we pick a value in the interval, say \(1\), and plug it into each factor to determine its sign in that interval: \(1-3=-2\), and \(1+5=6\). We don't have to worry about \(3x^2\) because it doesn't impact the sign of the product of the factors, always being positive. Making a sign chart can seem like a very time-consuming undertaking. However, once you have a little practice making them, you notice a pattern: if you organize the factors on the top from "most negative" to "most positive" from left-to-right, and similarly organize the intervals from "most negative" to "most positive" from the top down, then the positive and negative entries in the cells will follow a triangular pattern that is easy to fill in without having to check each cell manually.
