L'Hôpital's Rule

Many of the limits we have evaluated have been quotients where the numerator and denominator are "in competition with one another", and we are trying to see which wins (i.e. which is smaller/larger - see Limits II for some examples). We see that \[\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{3}{x} = 0\] since the denominator becomes very large, and the numerator is finite. Similarly, \[\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{x}{6} = \infty\] We have a similar situation in limits involving very small quantitites: \[\lim_{x \to 0^-}\frac{2}{x} = -\infty \hspace{10mm} \text{ and } \hspace{10mm} \lim_{x \to 0}\frac{x}{5} = 0\] since a fixed quantity divided by a very small negative number is a very large negative number, and a very small number divided by a fixed quantity approaches zero.

But what if both the numerator and denominator approach very small numbers, or very large numbers? That is, how do we evaluate limits like \[\lim_{x \to 1}\frac{\ln(x)}{x-1} \hspace{10mm} \text{ or } \hspace{10mm}\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{e^x}{x^2} \hspace{8mm} ?\] L'Hôpital's Rule is a rule which helps us evaluate limits of these, which we call "indeterminate forms":

Suppose \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable and \(g'(x) \neq 0\) near \(a\) (except possibly at \(a\)). Suppose that \[\lim_{x \to a}f(x) = 0 \hspace{3mm} \text{ and } \hspace{3mm} \lim_{x \to a}g(x) = 0\] \[\text{or } \hspace{5mm} \lim_{x \to a}f(x) = \pm \infty \hspace{3mm} \text{ and } \hspace{3mm} \lim_{x \to a}g(x) = \pm \infty\hspace{3mm}\] (That is, we have an indeterminate form of type \(\frac{0}{0}\) of type \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\)) Then \[\lim_{x \to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}\] if the limit on the right-hand side exists (or is \(\pm \infty\)).

To apply L'Hôpital's Rule, we must first confirm that we are dealing with an indeterminate form. For instance, \[\lim_{x \to 1}\frac{\ln(x)}{x-1}\] is an indeterminate form of type \(\frac{0}{0}\), so we can differentiate the numerator and denominator: \[\lim_{x \to 1}\frac{\ln(x)}{x-1} = \lim_{x \to 1}\frac{\frac{1}{x}}{1} = \lim_{x \to 1}\frac{1}{x} = \frac{1}{1} = 1\] After some practice evaluating limits, you will start to recognize a sort of "tier list" of functions in terms of how quickly they grow (that is, the limit as \(x \to \infty\)). Generally: \[\text{Logarithmic} < \text{Root} < \text{Polynomial} < \text{Exponential}\] This will help us predict the result of "which will win" in the contest between numerator and denominator. For instance, we will have to apply L'Hôpital's Rule twice to evaluate the following limit (as we will have an indeterminate form of type \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\) in both instances): \[\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{e^x}{x^2} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{e^x}{2x} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{e^x}{2} = \infty\] We can see from the preceding example that even \(\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{5634563x^{2524359}}{e^x} = 0\), as we will simply apply L'Hôpital's Rule repeatedly until the numerator "dies off" (see The Differentiation: A Survivor's Tale, a story from a favourite comic creator of mine, for a haunting tale of functions' relative experiences with differentiation).

L'Hôpital's Rule can be used to evaluate limits which we had previously used other techniques on. For instance, we previously evaluated infinite limits of rational functions as follows: \[\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{6x^4 - 9}{2x^4 + 1000x^3 + 3000} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\frac{6x^4}{x^4} - \frac{9}{x^4}}{\frac{2x^4}{x^4} + \frac{1000x^3}{x^4} + \frac{3000}{x^4}} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{6 - \frac{9}{x^4}}{2 + \frac{1000}{x} + \frac{3000}{x^4}} = \frac{6 - 0 + 0}{2 + 0} = \frac{6}{2} = 3\] However, we could have recognized that we started with an indeterminate form of type \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\), and differentiated repeatedly: \[\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{6x^4 - 9}{2x^4 + 1000x^3 + 3000} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{24x^3}{8x^3 + 3000x^2} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{72x^2}{24x^2} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{72}{24} = 3\] Once we've learned L'Hôpital's Rule, it can be tempting to treat it as a sledgehammer that we want to use on every limit we encounter. However, we must be cautious of two things:

  • Make sure that we have an indeterminate form, otherwise L'Hôpital's Rule can't be applied.
  • Sometimes, even if L'Hôpital's Rule can be applied, it doesn't necessarily mean it will help.
On the first point, you might glance at \[\lim_{x \to 0^+}\frac{\ln x}{x}\] and think to apply L'Hôpital's Rule. However, as \(x \to 0^+\), the numerator \(\ln x \to -\infty\), and the denominator \(x \to 0^+\). The fraction \(\frac{-\infty}{0}\) is not an indeterminate form. A very large negative number divided by a very small positive number is a very large negative number. So we immediately see that \[\lim_{x \to 0^+}\frac{\ln x}{x} = -\infty\] without needing to apply L'Hôpital's Rule.

On the second point, consider the following attempt to evaluate a limit, which is an indeterminate form of type \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\): \[\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{\sqrt{2x^2+5}}{3x-6} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{\frac{1}{2}(2x^2+5)^{-\frac{1}{2}}\cdot 4x}{3} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{4x}{3\sqrt{2x^2+5}} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{4}{3\cdot \frac{1}{2}(2x^2+5)^{-\frac{1}{2}}\cdot 4x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{8\sqrt{2x^2+5}}{12x}\] We aren't getting anywhere here: the linear and root will just keep swapping places as we repeatedly apply L'Hôpital's Rule. However, this problem can be solved by our more elementary strategy of dividing the numerator and denominator by the higher power in the smallest degree of the two (in this case, by \(x\)): \[\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{\sqrt{2x^2+5}}{3x-6} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\frac{1}{x}\sqrt{2x^2+5}}{\frac{3x}{x}-\frac{6}{x}} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{\sqrt{\frac{2x^2}{x^2}+\frac{5}{x^2}}}{3 - \frac{6}{x}} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{\sqrt{2 + \frac{5}{x^2}}}{3 - \frac{6}{x}} = \frac{\sqrt{2 - 0}}{3 - 0} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{3}\] It can sometimes be difficult to tell "when to stop" (i.e., when do we recognize whether or not applying L'Hôpital's Rule repeatedly is helping?). Answering the question "are we in a better position than we were in before?" is a skill that largely comes with the experience of evaluating many limits. For instance, consider the following: \[\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{5^x}{4^x} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{5^x \cdot \ln 5}{4^x \cdot \ln 4} = \frac{\ln 5}{\ln 4} \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{5^x}{4^x}\] That last step is the tricky part: recognizing that we have gotten something "larger" (not really: a number can't be larger than itself) than we started with after applying L'Hôpital's Rule, and that this would continue to happen with repeated applications. And so, \[\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{5^x}{4^x} = \infty\] (which you might have guessed to begin with, since the base in the numerator is larger than that in the denominator).

There are other indeterminate forms (products, differences, and powers), and in all of these cases the goal is the same: to rephrase the problem in terms of an indeterminate quotient, and then apply L'Hôpital's Rule.

We start with indeterminate products: those of the form \(0 \cdot \infty\). For instance, we rephrase the following as an indeterminate form of type \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\), and then apply L'Hôpital's Rule: \[\lim_{x \to \infty}x^3e^{-x^2} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{x^3}{e^{x^2}} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{3x^2}{e^{x^2}\cdot 2x} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{3x}{2e^{x^2}} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{3}{2e^{x^2}\cdot 2x} = 0\] Note that there were two choices we could have made in terms of how to rephrase it as a quotient: the other was as an indeterminate form of type \(\frac{0}{0}\): \[\lim_{x \to \infty}x^3e^{-x^2} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{e^{-x^2}}{x^{-3}} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{e^{-x^2}\cdot -2x}{-3x^{-4}} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{2}{3}x^5e^{-x^2}\] ...which is still an indeterminate form of type \(0 \cdot \infty\). That is, we aren't making any progress taking this approach. There comes a point where we must recognize that applying L'Hôpital's Rule has not improved our position (in fact it has worsened it here), and so we must backtrack and try the other avenue.

Next, indeterminate differences: those of the form \(\infty - \infty\). We handle these similarly - observe in the following we combine fractions with a common denominator to obtain an indeterminate form of type \(\frac{0}{0}\), and then apply L'Hôpital's Rule: \[\lim_{x \to \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)^-}(\sec x - \tan x) = \lim_{x \to \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)^-} \left(\frac{1}{\cos x}-\frac{\sin x}{\cos x}\right) = \lim_{x \to \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)^-}\frac{1 - \sin x}{\cos x} = \lim_{x \to \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)^-}\frac{-\cos x}{- \sin x} = \frac{0}{-1} = 0\]

Finally, indeterminate powers: those of the form \(0^0\), \(\infty^0\), or \(1^{\infty}\). We can deal with these by instead evaluating the limit of the natural logarithm of the limit's argument, so that we can take advantage of the laws of logarithms to rephrase the problem in terms of an indeterminate quotient. We then use this result to solve the original problem. For example, to solve the problem \[\lim_{x \to 0^+}(\tan 2x)^x\] we recognize we have an indeterminate form of type \(0^0\). We take the natural logarithm to solve a related problem: \[y = (\tan 2x)^x \implies \ln y = \ln\left((\tan 2x)^x\right) \implies \ln y = x\ln(\tan 2x)\] Then we take the limit of this, which is an indeterminate product of the form \(0 \cdot \infty\), so we turn it into an indeterminate quotient of the form \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\) and then apply L'Hôpital's Rule: \[\lim_{x \to 0^+}(x\ln(\tan 2x)) = \lim_{x \to 0^+}\frac{\ln(\tan 2x)}{x^{-1}} = \lim_{x \to 0^+}\frac{\frac{1}{\tan 2x} \cdot \sec^2(2x) \cdot 2}{-x^{-2}} = \lim_{x \to 0^+}\left(-2x^2 \cdot \frac{\cos 2x}{\sin 2x} \cdot \frac{1}{\cos^2(2x)}\right) = \lim_{x \to 0^+}\frac{-2x^2}{\sin 2x\cos 2x} = \lim_{x \to 0^+}\frac{2x}{\sin 2x}\frac{-x}{\cos 2x}\] Then recognizing that \(\lim_{x \to 0}\frac{2x}{\sin 2x} = 1\), \[=\lim_{x \to 0^+}\frac{-x}{\cos 2x} = \frac{0}{1} = 0\] Finally, we have just evaluated \(\lim_{x \to 0^+}\ln y\). So since \(y = e^{\ln y}\), \[\lim_{x \to 0^+}(\tan 2x)^x = \lim_{x \to 0^+}y = \lim_{x \to 0^+}e^{\ln y} = e^0 = 1\] Now you try!
