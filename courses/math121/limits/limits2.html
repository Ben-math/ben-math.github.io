Limits II

We next consider limits involving infinity. The first case we are interested in is when the particular way in which the limit doesn't exist is that the values become arbitrarily large (in either the positive/negative direction), in which case we write \[\lim_{x \to n}f(x) = \pm\infty\] In the second case, we are looking at the end behaviour of a function: \[\lim_{x \to \pm\infty}f(x)\] There are a number of things that can happen here: the function may become arbitrarily large/small (approach \(\pm\infty\)) or it may approach a particular value (this will correspond to a horizontal asymptote).

For example, in trying to evaluate \[\lim_{x \to 2}\frac{1}{x-2}\] we see no opportunity to cancel the \(x-2\) from the denominator. But we are still interested in the behaviour of the function near \(x = 2\). We consider the left- and right-hand limits. Observe that as \(x\) approaches 2 from the left, \(x-2\) becomes a very small negative value. That is, \[\lim_{x \to 2^-}\frac{1}{x-2} = -\infty\] Similarly, as \(x\) approaches 2 from the right, \(x-2\) becomes a very small positive value, so \[\lim_{x \to 2^+}\frac{1}{x-2} = \infty\] Since the two one-sided limits are not equal to one another, we simply say that the two sided limit \(\lim_{x \to 2}\frac{1}{x-2}\) does not exist. Visually:

If either of the one- or two-sided limits of a function \(f(x)\) approach \(\pm\infty\) at \(x = a\), then we say that \(f(x)\) has a vertical asymptote at the line \(x = a\).

We are also interested in the end behaviour of functions: that is, the limit as \(x \to \pm\infty\) (as we go all the way to the left or right). There are a number of possibilities. If \[\lim_{x \to -\infty}f(x) = L \hspace{3mm} \text{ or } \hspace{3mm} \lim_{x \to \infty}f(x) = L\] for some constant \(L\), then we say that \(f(x)\) has a horizontal asymptote at the line \(y = L\). For example, consider the function \[f(x) = \frac{6x^4 - 9}{2x^4 + 1000x^3 + 3000}\] At a glance, one might be tempted to say that the very large constant term and cubic coefficient in the denominator would "win" the contest between numerator and denominator, and thus in the limit as \(x \to \infty\), that the function approaches the \(y = 0\) line. However, one would be mistaken. Generally, evaluating limits as \(x \to \pm\infty\) involves determining which term "dominates". In the preceding example, as \(x\) becomes very large, the degree 4 terms dramatically outpace all others, and so they are the determining factor in the end behaviour. We can verify this directly by dividing all terms in the fraction by the highest power of \(x\) in the lowest degree out of the numerator/denominator (in this case they are both degree 4 polynomials, so we are dividing by \(x^4\). If one had been degree 4 and one degree 2, we would divide by \(x^2\)): \[\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{6x^4 - 9}{2x^4 + 1000x^3 + 3000} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\frac{6x^4}{x^4} - \frac{9}{x^4}}{\frac{2x^4}{x^4} + \frac{1000x^3}{x^4} + \frac{3000}{x^4}} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{6 - \frac{9}{x^4}}{2 + \frac{1000}{x} + \frac{3000}{x^4}}\] We can see that every term in the fraction approaches zero as \(x \to \infty\) with the exception of the two constant terms, so \[= \frac{6 - 0}{2 + 0 + 0} = \frac{6}{2} = 3\] and so we have a horizontal asymptote at the line \(y = 3\). Experimenting with this technique on a variety of different degree numerators/denominators in a rational function, one can discover the following result (where \(f(x)\) has lead term \(ax^n\) and \(g(x)\) has lead term \(bx^m\): \[\lim_{x \to \infty}h(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } n < m\\ \frac{a}{b} & \text{if } n = m\\ \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{a}{b}x^{n-m} & \text{if } n > m \end{cases}\] That is, if the degree of the numerator is less than the degree of the denominator, we have a horizontal asymptote at \(y=0\); if the degrees are equal, then there is a horizontal asymptote at the ratio of the coefficients, \(y=\frac{a}{b}\); and finally if the degree of the numerator is greater than that of the denominator, the limit approaches \(\pm\infty\). In the last case, we have to be careful in determining the sign: it will be dependent on whether the difference of the degrees is an even or odd number (try experimenting with it to discover this).

For a simpler example, consider the end behaviour of a polynomial. It will be determined by two things: the sign and parity (even or odd) of the lead term \(ax^n\). If \(n\) is even we have identical end behaviour in both directions: \[\lim_{x \to \pm\infty}f(x) = \infty \hspace{2mm} \text{if } a > 0 \hspace{3mm} \text{ and } \lim_{x \to \pm\infty}f(x) = -\infty \hspace{2mm} \text{if } a < 0\] If \(n\) is odd we have have oppositive end behaviour in each direction: \[\lim_{x \to \infty}f(x) = \infty \hspace{2mm} \text{ and } \lim_{x \to -\infty}f(x) = -\infty \hspace{2mm} \text{if } a > 0 \hspace{1cm} \text{ and } \hspace{1cm} \lim_{x \to \infty}f(x) = -\infty \hspace{2mm} \text{ and } \lim_{x \to -\infty}f(x) = \infty \hspace{2mm} \text{if } a < 0 \]

We end with a couple more challenging examples. First, the same strategy we applied to a ratio of two polynomials could also be applied to a fraction whose numerator and denominator contains powers of anything, not just \(x\) (so long as the base is the same). For example, another "which will win: numerator or denominator?" problem: \[\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{2 \cdot 5^{-6x} - 3 \cdot 5^{4x}}{6 \cdot 5^{3x}} = \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{\frac{2 \cdot 5^{-6x}}{5^{3x}} - \frac{3 \cdot 5^{4x}}{5^{3x}}}{\frac{6 \cdot5^{3x}}{5^{3x}}} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2 \cdot 5^{-9x} - 3 \cdot 5^x}{6}\] We see that as \(x \to \infty\), \(2 \cdot 5^{-9x} \to 0\) and \(-3 \cdot 5^x \to -\infty\), so the limit \[= -\infty\]

Finally, what if we have a composition of functions? We evaluate the limit from the inside-out. Consider: \[\lim_{x \to \infty} \sin\left(\frac{2\pi x^8 + x^2 - 9}{12x^8 - 3x^6}\right)\] Using the theorem stated earlier, since the numerator and denominator have the same degree, we know \[\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{2\pi x^8 + x^2 - 9}{12x^8 - 3x^6} = \frac{2\pi}{12} = \frac{\pi}{6}\] So \[\lim_{x \to \infty} \sin\left(\frac{2\pi x^8 + x^2 - 9}{12x^8 - 3x^6}\right) = \sin\left(\frac{\pi}{6}\right) = \frac{1}{2}\] Now you try!
