Fractions/Decimals/Percent Activity

Somehwat confusingly, "rational numbers" need not necessarily be written in the form of a fraction. We have seen that all integers are themselves rational numbers, so in this case it is obvious: \[5 = \frac{5}{1}\] But what about decimal numbers, and percentages? Are they rational numbers? The answer is not obvious, but it turns out that any number which can be written with a terminating or repeating decimal expansion is a rational number, and we can demonstrate this by converting them into fractions.

Converting fractions to decimals:

This is a simple conversion if you have a calculator: simply divide the numerator by the denominator. If you don't, let the long division begin! See below converting 3/8 into a decimal:

\[ \require{enclose} \begin{array}{rr} & 0.375 \hspace{1mm}\\ 8 & \enclose{longdiv}{3.000} \\ & \underline{- 2.4} \hspace{7mm}\\ & .60 \hspace{4.5mm}\\ & \underline{- .56 \hspace{1mm}}\hspace{3.5mm}\\ & .040 \hspace{2mm}\\ & \underline{- .040 \hspace{2mm}}\\ & .000 \hspace{2mm} \end{array} \]

Sometimes the division doesn't result in a terminating decimal. Sometimes we enter an endless cycle: these repeating decimals can be found by dividing until the first repetition after the decimal point occurs. See below a worked example using the flexible scaffold (partial quotients) division method, to convert 1/7 into a decimal:

\[ \require{enclose} \begin{array}{rr} & 0.0000001 \hspace{1mm}\\ & 0.0000070 \hspace{1mm}\\ & 0.0000500 \hspace{1mm}\\ & 0.0008000 \hspace{1mm}\\ & 0.0020000 \hspace{1mm}\\ & 0.0400000 \hspace{1mm}\\ & 0.1000000 \hspace{1mm}\\ 7 & \enclose{longdiv}{1.0000000} \\ & \underline{- .7000000} \hspace{1mm}\\ & .3000000 \hspace{1mm}\\ & \underline{- .2800000 \hspace{1mm}}\\ & .040 \hspace{2mm}\\ & \underline{- .040 \hspace{2mm}}\\ & .000 \hspace{2mm} \end{array} \]
