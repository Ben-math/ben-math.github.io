Normal Distribution

The normal distribution is continuous, and easily the most important probability distribution. This is because it occurs so often naturally. Many things you might think of: heights, weights, test grades, etc. tend to be normally distributed.

The continuous random variable \(X\) is said to be normally distributed with parameters \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) if and only if its pdf is given by \[f(x;\mu,\sigma^2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2}\] The two parameters are named as usual: \(\mu\) is the mean, and \(\sigma\) is the standard deviation.

Finding areas beneath this function is even beyond the reach of calculus. We will use tables to work with it.

The normal distribution is the well-known bell-shaped curve, symmetric about its mean \(\mu\). For instance, with \(\mu = 3\), see below some examples for various values of \(\sigma^2\) (\({\color{green}0.5}, {\color{red}1}, {\color{blue}2}\)): INSERT FIGURE HERE
That is, changing \(\mu\) translates the graph to the left or right, and increasing \(\sigma\) flattens the curve out.

The standard normal distribution is given by setting \(\mu = 0\) and \(\sigma = 1\). This is a well-understood distribution which we have constructed tables to work with. We also have a simple transformation to go from any normal distribution to the standard one:

If \(X\) has normal distribution \(f(x;\mu,\sigma)\), then \[Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\] has the standard normal distribution.

These values, called \(z\)-scores, tell us how many standard deviations above (to the right) or below (to the left) of the mean a value of \(x\) is.

Note that if \(x\) is above the mean, its \(z\)-score will be positive, and if it is below, its \(z\)-score will be negative. Suppose \(X\) is a normally distributed random variable with mean \(\mu = 18\) and standard deviation \(\sigma = 6\). Then the \(z\)-score of \(x = 9\) is \[z = \frac{9 - 18}{6} = -1.5\] So the data value 9 is 1.5 standard deviations below the mean.

Conversely, if a data value \(x\) has a \(z\)-score of 3, then it is 3 standard deviations above the mean: \[x = \mu + 3\sigma = 18 + 3(6) = 36\]

For example, suppose height is normally distributed, with a mean of 168 cm and a standard deviation of 7 cm. If a person is 182 cm tall, then their \(z\)-score is \[z = \frac{182 - 168}{7} = 2\] If a person has a \(z\)-score of \(-4\), they are \[x = \mu -4\sigma = 168 - 4(7) = 140 \text{ cm tall}\]

There is an empirical rule which states that, for any normally distributed random variable \(X\) with mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\):

  • About 68% of the data lie within one standard deviation of the mean. That is, their \(z\)-scores are between \(-1\) and 1
  • About 95% of the data lie within two standard deviations of the mean. That is, their \(z\)-scores are between \(-2\) and 2
  • About 99.7% of the data lie within three standard deviations of the mean. That is, their \(z\)-scores are between \(-3\) and 3
So, in our previous example with \(\mu = 168\) cm and \(\sigma = 7\) cm, we have:
  • About 68% of people have heights between 161 cm and 175 cm
  • About 95% of people have heights between 154 cm and 182 cm
  • About 99.7% of people have heights between 147 cm and 189 cm
